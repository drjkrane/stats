Study Design: Which Type of Evidence Can You Trust?

Different study designs answer "does this work?" with very different amounts of confidence. The hierarchy exists because some designs control for bias and chance far better than others — not because one is more "scientific" in spirit, but because the design itself rules out more alternative explanations.

Worked example

Imagine the same clinical question — does a particular medication reduce the rate of a serious complication — examined by different study types. This is illustrative, not real published data, but shows the typical pattern:

DesignTypical findingWhy the result can mislead
Case report (1 patient)"Patient improved"No comparison group at all
Retrospective cohortRelative risk 0.75Confounding by indication — healthier patients more likely to receive the drug
Prospective cohort, adjustedRelative risk 0.82Unmeasured confounders can still distort the estimate
Randomised controlled trialRelative risk 0.70 (95% CI 0.62–0.79)Randomisation balances confounders — the most trustworthy single estimate
Meta-analysis of several RCTsRelative risk 0.71 (95% CI 0.66–0.76)Most precise, but only as good as the trials feeding into it

What to watch out for

  • "Retrospective" doesn't mean "wrong," but it does mean extra scrutiny. Ask who decided which patients got which treatment, and whether that decision could be linked to prognosis.
  • A bigger sample doesn't fix a weaker design. A retrospective cohort with 50,000 patients is still vulnerable to the same confounding as one with 500 — size buys precision, not validity.
  • Check whether the comparator is fair. Many "negative" observational studies compare a new treatment only against patients too sick to have received it.
  • RCTs aren't automatically perfect either — check for adequate blinding, allocation concealment, and whether the population studied resembles your own patients.

Randomised Trials: What Randomisation Actually Buys You

Randomisation's entire purpose is to make treatment groups comparable on everything — measured and unmeasured — except the treatment itself, on average. It doesn't guarantee any single trial's groups are perfectly balanced; it guarantees that imbalances are due to chance, which standard statistics can account for.

Worked example

A trial randomises 200 patients 1:1 to Treatment vs Control. Purely by chance, the treatment arm ends up slightly older (mean age 61 vs 58) and with slightly more advanced-stage disease at baseline:

Treatment (n=100)Control (n=100)
Mean age6158
Stage III/IV (%)42%35%

This is a normal, expected outcome of randomisation with a moderate sample size — it isn't evidence the randomisation "failed." Because the imbalance arose by chance rather than by clinician or patient choice, the trial's primary analysis (intention-to-treat) remains valid; a pre-specified adjusted analysis controlling for age and stage can also be reported as a sensitivity check.

What to watch out for

  • Allocation concealment is not the same as blinding. Concealment stops the recruiter from knowing/influencing the next assignment; blinding stops participants/assessors from knowing which arm a patient is in after allocation. Trials can have one without the other.
  • "Randomised" doesn't guarantee balance in any one trial — chance imbalances happen, especially in smaller trials; check the baseline characteristics table rather than assuming.
  • Intention-to-treat (analyse as randomised) is the standard primary analysis for superiority trials — switching to per-protocol analysis after seeing the data, or only in the arm that struggled with adherence, can reintroduce the very bias randomisation was meant to prevent.
  • Stratified randomisation (e.g. by centre, by disease stage) reduces the chance of imbalance on known important factors — but only for the factors explicitly built into the randomisation scheme.
  • A non-inferiority trial needs different handling entirely — poor adherence or loss to follow-up can make two genuinely different treatments look "non-inferior" by diluting any true difference (see the Trial Design Advisor at the start of this section (01a) for more).

Observational Studies: Odds Ratios Are Not Relative Risks

Cohort studies typically report relative risk; case-control studies are usually limited to reporting odds ratios (since cohort size and disease risk aren't both knowable from a case-control design). The two measures agree closely when the outcome is rare — and diverge meaningfully when it isn't.

Worked example

A cohort study comparing an exposure's association with a fairly common outcome (occurring in 10–40% of the cohort, not a rare event):

Outcome presentOutcome absentRisk
Exposed (n=1000)40060040%
Unexposed (n=1000)10090010%
Relative riskOdds ratio
4.006.00

The true risk is 4 times higher in the exposed group — but the odds ratio, often reported as if interchangeable with relative risk, suggests a 6-fold difference. With a common outcome, the odds ratio systematically overstates the relative risk; the gap widens further as the outcome becomes more common still.

What to watch out for

  • Check whether the paper's outcome is actually rare (a common rough guideline: under ~10%) before treating a reported odds ratio as a stand-in for relative risk.
  • Case-control studies cannot report incidence or relative risk directly — only the odds ratio is calculable from that design, because cases and controls are selected by outcome status, not sampled from the underlying population at their natural disease rate.
  • Confounding by indication is the dominant threat in observational comparisons of treatments — see the worked Simpson's-paradox example under Confounding (09) for how severe this can get.
  • The Bradford Hill criteria (strength, consistency, dose-response, plausibility, temporality, and others) are a useful checklist for judging whether an observed association in cohort data is likely to be causal — no single criterion is decisive on its own.
  • Reverse causation is easy to miss — does the exposure really cause the outcome, or could early/subclinical disease be influencing exposure status (e.g. patients who feel unwell exercising less, rather than inactivity causing illness)?

Bias: When the Method Itself Distorts the Answer

Bias is any systematic (not random) error that pushes a result in a particular direction — unlike random chance, it doesn't average out with a bigger sample. It comes from how patients were selected, how data was measured, or how analyses were chosen, rather than from the underlying biology.

Worked example: selection bias from recruitment route

A trial recruits the same eligible condition through two different channels:

Recruitment routenResponse rate
Hospital clinic (sicker patients)40035%
Community self-referral (healthier, more motivated)60052%

Pooling these naively into one "trial population" gives an overall response rate of 45.2% — but that number describes neither group accurately, and depends entirely on the (somewhat arbitrary) mix of recruitment routes. A different clinic-to-community ratio next time would shift the headline number even if nothing about the treatment changed.

What to watch out for

  • Ask "who was missing?" as often as "who was included." Patients lost to follow-up, those too unwell to consent, or those who declined are rarely a random subset.
  • Recall bias — in case-control or retrospective designs, people with the outcome (e.g. disease) often remember or report past exposures differently from those without it.
  • Measurement bias — if outcome assessors know which group a patient is in, even unconscious differences in how they assess outcomes can shift results (this is exactly why blinding matters).
  • Immortal time bias — classifying patients by something that can only be known after they've survived long enough to have it observed (covered in detail under Survival Analysis, 07f).
  • Bias usually has a predictable direction once you know the mechanism — ask not just "is there bias?" but "which way would it push the result, and is that consistent with what we're seeing?"

Sample Size & Power: How Many Patients Do You Actually Need?

Power is the chance your study will detect a real effect, if one truly exists, at the size you're looking for. Run a trial that's too small, and a genuine benefit can easily look like "no difference" simply because there wasn't enough data to tell signal from noise.

Worked example

A trial comparing two treatments on a continuous outcome (e.g. % tumour shrinkage), expecting a mean difference of 5 points with a standard deviation of 10, wanting the standard 80% power at a two-sided 5% significance level:

InputValue
Expected difference5 points
Standard deviation10 points
Significance level (α)0.05, two-sided
Target power80%
Required sample size63 patients per arm (126 total)

Halving the expected difference to 2.5 points (same SD) doesn't double the required sample size — it roughly quadruples it, because sample size scales with the square of the effect size. This is the single most common reason underpowered trials happen: the assumed effect size was optimistic.

What to watch out for

  • The assumed effect size drives everything. A trial powered for a large effect will systematically miss smaller (but still clinically meaningful) ones.
  • "Non-significant" is not the same as "no effect." An underpowered trial showing p=0.29 (as in the survival worked example, 07e) may still reflect a real effect the study simply wasn't big enough to confirm.
  • Don't recalculate sample size off observed pilot variability without caution — small pilot studies give unstable SD estimates, which can make a "powered" follow-on trial still underpowered in practice.
  • Power calculations assume the stated analysis will actually be run as specified. Switching primary endpoints, adding interim looks without adjustment, or analysing per-protocol instead of intention-to-treat can all silently erode the power you thought you had.
  • Use the Sample Size & Power Calculator in this section to check different scenarios (means, proportions, survival/log-rank) before committing to a design.

Small Samples: Standard Tests Can Quietly Give the Wrong Answer

Many common tests (chi-squared, the standard t-test's normal approximations) rely on having "enough" data for their underlying approximations to hold. With small samples, the approximation doesn't just become imprecise — it can flip a result from significant to non-significant, or the reverse.

Worked example

A small pilot trial, 8 patients per arm, comparing response rates:

RespondedDid not respond
Treatment (n=6)51
Control (n=12)39
Methodp-valueConclusion
Chi-squared (standard approximation)0.065Not significant
Fisher's exact test0.043Significant

The expected cell counts here are as low as 2.7 — well under the conventional rule-of-thumb minimum of 5 needed for the chi-squared approximation to be trustworthy. Fisher's exact test computes the exact probability directly rather than relying on a large-sample approximation, and here it changes the conclusion.

What to watch out for

  • Check expected cell counts before trusting a chi-squared p-value — if any expected count is under 5, prefer Fisher's exact test.
  • Confidence intervals matter more, not less, with small samples — they'll be wide, and that width is itself important honest information, not a flaw to hide.
  • Simon two-stage designs let early-phase single-arm oncology trials stop early for futility without committing the full planned sample — a standard approach when only a handful of patients are feasible to enrol.
  • Bayesian methods with informative priors can extract more from limited data than frequentist methods, but the prior's influence needs to be transparently justified and reported — an aggressive prior can manufacture apparent precision that isn't really there.
  • Don't pool numerically small subgroups just to get "enough" data without checking they're clinically similar enough to combine — small samples are tempting to patch over this way, but doing so can reintroduce confounding.

Variable Types & Choosing a Test: Get This Wrong, and Everything After It Is Wrong

Before any statistical test can be chosen, you need to know what kind of variable you're dealing with — continuous, categorical, or ordinal — because the wrong test on the wrong variable type gives an answer that looks plausible but means nothing.

Worked example

A study collects three outcomes on the same patients: tumour size in mm (continuous), tumour response category — complete/partial/stable/progressive (ordinal), and biopsy-confirmed recurrence yes/no (binary categorical). Each needs a genuinely different summary and test:

VariableTypeRight way to summariseWrong (but common) approach
Tumour size (mm)ContinuousMean ± SD (if roughly symmetric) or median + IQR (if skewed)Reporting only the mean when the data is heavily skewed
Response categoryOrdinalCounts/percentages per category, or rank-based test (e.g. trend test)Treating CR/PR/SD/PD as a 1-2-3-4 continuous scale and averaging it
Recurrence yes/noBinary categoricalProportions, chi-squared/Fisher's exact, risk ratiosRunning a t-test on a 0/1 coded variable

What to watch out for

  • Don't average ordinal categories as if they were numbers — the gap between "complete response" and "partial response" isn't necessarily the same size as the gap between "stable" and "progressive."
  • Check skew before reporting a mean. If the mean and median are noticeably different, the mean alone is misleading — report both, or use the median with IQR.
  • Small counts in any category (especially under 5 expected) usually mean an exact test (e.g. Fisher's) is safer than the usual chi-squared approximation.
  • Paired vs independent matters as much as the variable type. The same two columns of numbers need a completely different test depending on whether they came from the same patients (e.g. before/after) or different ones.
  • Use the Statistical Test Selector tool in section 02b to work through these decisions for your specific data rather than guessing from memory.

Visualising Data: The Same Numbers Can Tell Different Stories

How a result is plotted can change how convincing it looks, even when the underlying numbers haven't changed at all. A handful of common chart choices are responsible for most accidental (or deliberate) visual exaggeration in clinical figures.

Worked example

A bar chart comparing two treatments' response rates: 42% vs 38% — a real but modest 4-point difference.

Y-axis choiceVisual impression
Axis from 0% to 100%Bars look nearly identical — accurately reflects a small difference
Axis truncated to 35%–45%One bar looks roughly twice the height of the other — same data, dramatically different impression

Nothing about the underlying 42% vs 38% comparison changed — only where the y-axis started. This single design choice is the most common way response-rate or survival bar charts are made to look more dramatic than the data supports.

What to watch out for

  • Always check the y-axis range on a bar chart before judging the size of a difference — a truncated axis is the single most common way to visually exaggerate a small effect.
  • Kaplan-Meier curves need a number-at-risk table beneath them — without it, a dramatic-looking late-curve plateau or separation might be based on just a handful of remaining patients (see 07b for the full explanation).
  • Pie charts are poor at conveying precise comparisons between similar-sized categories — a simple bar chart or even a table is usually clearer for clinical audiences.
  • Error bars need a label. SD, SEM, and 95% CI all look like "error bars" but represent very different amounts of uncertainty — SEM bars in particular look reassuringly narrow but understate the spread of individual patient values.
  • Dual y-axes inviting visual comparison of two unrelated scales (e.g. overlaying a drug's sales on a clinical outcome) can imply a relationship that isn't statistically tested at all.

Probability: Why a "95% Accurate" Test Can Still Mislead You

The single most clinically important probability idea is this: a test's accuracy (sensitivity and specificity) is a fixed property of the test, but what a positive or negative result actually means for a given patient depends heavily on how common the condition is in that patient's population — and that's where intuition usually fails.

Worked example

A biomarker test for a condition with 2% prevalence, with 95% sensitivity and 90% specificity — both numbers that sound highly reliable. Testing 10,000 people from this population:

Disease present (200)Disease absent (9,800)
Test positive190 (true positive)980 (false positive)
Test negative10 (false negative)8,820 (true negative)

Of the 1,170 people who test positive, only 190 (16.2%) actually have the condition. A "95% accurate" test produces a positive predictive value of just 16% in a low-prevalence population — most positive results here are false alarms, purely because the disease is rare to begin with.

What to watch out for

  • Sensitivity and specificity don't change with prevalence — PPV and NPV do, dramatically. The same test can be genuinely useful for confirming disease in a high-risk referral population and nearly useless as a population-wide screening tool.
  • Always ask "positive predictive value in what population?" before judging a test's real-world usefulness — the number is meaningless without that context.
  • Conditional probability runs in one direction only. P(positive test | disease) — sensitivity — is not the same number as P(disease | positive test) — PPV — and confusing the two is the single most common statistical error in clinical reasoning about tests.
  • Use the Diagnostic Test Calculator (06) to compute PPV/NPV at whatever prevalence matches your actual clinical setting, not just the study's sampling fractions.

Hypothesis Testing & p-values: What the Number Actually Tells You

A p-value answers one narrow question: if there were truly no difference between groups, how unusual would data this extreme be? It does not tell you the probability the treatment works, how large the effect is, or how clinically important it is — that's what the effect size and confidence interval are for.

Worked example

Two treatment arms, 8 patients each, comparing % tumour shrinkage:

Standard treatment12, 15, 9, 18, 14, 11, 16, 13
New treatment20, 24, 18, 27, 22, 19, 25, 21
Mean (Standard)Mean (New)Mean difference95% CIp-value
13.5%22.0%8.5 points5.3 to 11.7<0.0001
Reading this correctly The right sentence is: "the new treatment produced 8.5 points more shrinkage on average (95% CI 5.3–11.7), a difference very unlikely to arise from chance alone (p<0.0001)." The CI is doing the clinically important work here — it tells you the difference is probably meaningfully large, not just statistically detectable. The p-value alone would only tell you it's "probably real," without saying whether 8.5 points (versus, say, 0.5 points) matters to a patient.

What to watch out for

  • Statistically significant ≠ clinically meaningful. A huge sample can make a trivially small, irrelevant difference reach p<0.05.
  • p=0.06 is not "wrong" or "trending toward significance" — it crossed an arbitrary threshold, nothing more. Report the actual CI and let the reader judge.
  • One-sided vs two-sided tests change the threshold — pre-specify which you're using before seeing the data, not after.
  • p-values from multiple comparisons need correction (see Multiple Testing, 05) — running the same test 20 times and reporting only the significant one is a guaranteed way to manufacture false positives.
  • A non-significant result in a small study is not proof of "no effect" — it may simply reflect inadequate power (see 01i).

Multiple Testing: Why Running 20 Tests Finds "Significance" Even With Nothing Real Going On

Run enough statistical tests on the same data, and some will reach p<0.05 by chance alone — that's not a flaw in any individual test, it's basic probability. Multiple-testing correction exists to keep the overall false-positive rate under control when many comparisons are made at once.

Worked example

A genomic study tests 20 candidate biomarkers for association with treatment response, generating 20 p-values ranging from 0.001 to 0.88:

ApproachNumber "significant"
No correction (p < 0.05)8 of 20
Bonferroni correction1 of 20
Benjamini-Hochberg (FDR)2 of 20

Without correction, 8 of 20 biomarkers look "significant" — but with 20 independent tests run at α=0.05, roughly 1 false positive is expected purely by chance even if none of the biomarkers are truly associated with response. After correction, only 1–2 biomarkers survive — those are the ones worth taking forward for validation.

What to watch out for

  • "How many tests did you really run?" is the key question — including ones not reported. Selectively reporting only the significant comparisons (without disclosing the rest) defeats the purpose of any correction.
  • Bonferroni is conservative (controls the chance of even one false positive across all tests) — appropriate for confirmatory claims, but can bury real effects in exploratory work with many tests.
  • FDR (Benjamini-Hochberg) is the standard choice for exploratory/discovery work (e.g. -omics, biomarker screens) — it accepts a small, known proportion of false positives among the "significant" results in exchange for more power.
  • Subgroup analyses count as multiple tests too. Testing a trial's primary endpoint in 10 subgroups without correction or pre-specification is one of the most common ways spurious "significant" subgroup effects get reported.
  • Use the Multiple Testing Correction tool in this section to apply Bonferroni, Holm, and BH to your own p-values directly.

Sensitivity and specificity describe how well a test performs among people whose true disease status you already know — useful for comparing tests, but not directly the number a clinician needs when facing one patient's actual result. That number is the predictive value, and it depends on more than the test itself.

  • PPV and NPV only apply directly to a population with the same prevalence as the one tested. Foundations: Why Survival Data Needs Its Own Methods

Survival analysis (also called time-to-event analysis) handles outcomes defined by two pieces of information together: whether an event happened, and how long it took. The event doesn't have to be death — in oncology it's commonly overall survival (OS), progression-free survival (PFS), disease-free survival (DFS), or time to local recurrence. The same machinery applies to any "time until X" outcome: time to hospital discharge, time to infection, time to implant failure.

The problem ordinary statistics can't solve: censoring

In almost every real follow-up study, some patients haven't had the event by the time you analyse the data. A patient enrolled 3 months ago and still alive hasn't "failed to have an event" — their true survival time is simply unknown but at least 3 months. This is censoring, and it's the central problem survival analysis exists to solve.

TypeDefinitionTypical cause
Right-censoringTrue event time is known to be after the last observed timeStudy ends; patient lost to follow-up; alive at last contact
Left-censoringTrue event time is known to be before the first observed timeEvent already occurred before screening (rare in trials)
Interval-censoringEvent is known to have occurred somewhere between two visitsPeriodic scans/visits rather than continuous monitoring

Right-censoring is by far the most common in clinical trials, and it's what the standard methods (Kaplan-Meier, log-rank, Cox) are built around. Two assumptions matter enormously and are worth stating explicitly:

  • Non-informative censoring — the reason a patient is censored must be unrelated to their actual prognosis. If sicker patients drop out of follow-up more often (informative censoring), every method on this page becomes biased.
  • Independent censoring — censoring time and event time are statistically independent, conditional on any covariates in the model.
Common pitfall Simply excluding censored patients, or treating "censored" as a third outcome category in a chi-squared test, both throw away real information and typically bias estimates — usually toward overestimating risk, since patients followed for a long time without an event are disproportionately removed.

Why you can't just use mean survival time or ordinary regression

Two specific reasons ordinary linear regression on survival time fails:

  1. You don't know the outcome for censored patients. A patient censored at 8 months might survive to month 9, or to month 90 — treating 8 as their "value" understates true survival, sometimes severely.
  2. Survival times are never normally distributed. They're bounded at zero, typically right-skewed, and the variance usually changes over time — violating the core assumptions of ordinary least squares.

Survival methods solve this by modelling the probability of surviving past time t, rather than the raw time itself, and by using every patient's contributed follow-up time up to the point they are censored or have the event — known as the risk set at each time point. This single idea — using the risk set, rather than discarding censored patients — underlies the Kaplan-Meier estimator, the log-rank test, and Cox regression alike.

The two core functions

Survival function
S(t) = P(T > t) — the probability of being event-free beyond time t
Hazard function
h(t) = limΔt→0 P(t ≤ T < t+Δt | T ≥ t) / Δt — the instantaneous event rate at time t, among those still at risk

S(t) starts at 1 (everyone event-free at time zero) and decreases monotonically. The hazard h(t) is not a probability — it can exceed 1 — and describes risk per unit time, conditional on having survived this far. The two are mathematically linked: S(t) = exp(−∫h(u)du from 0 to t). Most of survival analysis is really about modelling and comparing hazards, because the hazard is what changes (or doesn't) with treatment, biomarkers, or stage — S(t) is simply its cumulative consequence.

Why this matters for trial design Whether the hazard is roughly constant, rising, or falling over time has direct consequences for the methods later in this section — particularly whether the proportional hazards assumption (07c) is plausible, and whether a single hazard ratio is even the right summary measure (07f, on RMST).

The Kaplan-Meier Estimator

The Kaplan-Meier (KM) method estimates S(t) directly from the data without assuming any particular shape for the hazard — it's a non-parametric estimator, and it is the standard way survival curves are presented in clinical trial publications.

The logic, step by step

At each time a death/event occurs, KM asks: "of everyone still being followed (the risk set) at this exact moment, what fraction survived past it?" It then multiplies these survival fractions together across all event times up to t — this is why it's also called the product-limit estimator.

Kaplan-Meier estimator
Ŝ(t) = ∏i: tᵢ≤t (1 − dᵢ/nᵢ)
where, at each observed event time tᵢ: nᵢ = number at risk just before tᵢ, dᵢ = number of events exactly at tᵢ

Censored patients leave the risk set at the time they're censored but don't count as a death — they simply stop contributing to nᵢ from that point onward. This is exactly the "use the risk set, don't discard censored patients" principle from the foundations page (07), made concrete.

Step-function shape, censoring marks, and the at-risk table

A KM curve is a step function — flat between event times, dropping vertically at each one. Censored observations are conventionally marked with a small tick (+) on the curve at the censoring time, without a drop. Any published KM figure should be accompanied by a number-at-risk table beneath the x-axis, showing how many patients remain under observation at regular intervals — this is what lets a reader judge how reliable the tail of the curve is (curves based on 2–3 remaining patients are far noisier than the steep early drops based on a full risk set).

Common pitfall Reading a KM curve's right-hand tail as if it were as reliable as the left-hand side. Once the risk set has thinned to a handful of patients, a single event or a single censoring can swing Ŝ(t) substantially — always check the at-risk numbers before trusting a late-curve plateau or crossing.

Confidence intervals around Ŝ(t)

Point estimates of Ŝ(t) are usually reported with a 95% CI, most commonly via Greenwood's formula for the variance:

Greenwood's variance formula
Var[Ŝ(t)] = Ŝ(t)² × Σi: tᵢ≤t dᵢ / [nᵢ(nᵢ − dᵢ)]

The CI is then Ŝ(t) ± 1.96 × √Var[Ŝ(t)] (often computed on a transformed, e.g. log-log, scale to keep the bounds within [0,1] — most software does this by default).

The Log-rank Test

The log-rank test answers one specific question: do two (or more) survival curves differ? It's the standard primary-analysis test for comparing survival between randomised arms, and it underlies the p-value reported alongside almost every published KM curve in oncology.

The logic: observed vs. expected deaths

At every time an event occurs anywhere in the combined dataset, the log-rank test builds a small 2×2 table of who was at risk and who had the event, in each arm. Under the null hypothesis (identical survival in both arms), the number of events in Arm A at that moment is expected to be proportional to Arm A's share of the combined risk set. Summing the gap between what was observed and what was expected across every event time gives the test statistic.

Expected events in Arm A at time tᵢ, under H₀
Eᵢ(A) = dᵢ × nᵢ(A) / nᵢ(total)
Log-rank chi-squared statistic (1 d.f.)
χ² = (O(A) − E(A))² / V   where   V = Σᵢ [dᵢ(nᵢ−dᵢ) nᵢ(A) nᵢ(B)] / [nᵢ²(nᵢ−1)]

This is the same Mantel-Haenszel machinery used elsewhere in epidemiology for combining 2×2 tables across strata — here, each "stratum" is a single point in time.

This is precisely why the Sample Size & Power tool (01i) matters before a trial like this is even designed.

Key assumptions

  • Proportional hazards — the standard log-rank test is most powerful when the hazard ratio between arms is roughly constant over time. If curves cross, the test can lose power even with a real difference present (see 07e for an alternative in that situation).
  • Non-informative censoring — carried over from the foundational assumptions in 07.
  • Independence between patients — violated by clustered data (e.g. multiple patients per surgeon/centre) unless explicitly modelled (see the frailty models on 07g).

Cox Proportional Hazards Regression

The log-rank test answers "is there a difference?" Cox regression answers "how big is the difference, adjusted for other variables, and is it consistent over time?" It's the workhorse model of clinical survival analysis — almost every published hazard ratio (HR) for a clinical outcome comes from a Cox model.

The model

Cox proportional hazards model
h(t | X) = h₀(t) × exp(β₁X₁ + β₂X₂ + … + βₚXₚ)

h₀(t) is the baseline hazard — an unspecified function of time, shared by everyone — and each exp(βⱼ) is the hazard ratio associated with a one-unit increase in Xⱼ, holding the other covariates fixed. The model is called semi-parametric: the covariate effects are estimated parametrically, but the baseline hazard's shape over time is left completely unspecified. This is Cox's key insight (Cox, 1972) — you can estimate the βⱼ without ever having to model h₀(t) at all, via the partial likelihood.

Why "proportional hazards"?

Because h₀(t) cancels out when comparing any two patients, the ratio of their hazards is constant over time — it doesn't depend on t:

h(t | X=1) / h(t | X=0) = exp(β)   — constant for all t

This is both the model's main strength (a single HR summarises the entire follow-up period) and its main fragility (if the true hazard ratio changes over time — e.g. early surgical risk versus later recurrence risk — a single HR can be a misleading summary; see "checking the assumption" below).

Worked example, continued: fitting the Cox model

Fitting a Cox model with a single binary covariate (armB = 1 for Treatment, 0 for Control) to the same 16-patient dataset used on the previous two pages, via maximum partial likelihood:

ParameterEstimate
Coefficient β (armB)−0.698
Hazard ratio, exp(β)0.50
SE(β)0.681
95% CI for HR0.13 – 1.89
Wald p-value0.31
Reading this result HR = 0.50 means the instantaneous risk of an event at any given moment is roughly half as high in Arm B as in Arm A, throughout follow-up. This is a substantial-looking effect size — but the 95% CI (0.13 to 1.89) spans both a dramatic benefit and a near-doubling of risk, and the p-value (0.31) is far from significant. This is the same underlying conclusion as the log-rank test on 07b (consistent direction, not statistically significant) — as it should be, since with a single binary covariate, the Cox score test and the log-rank test are asymptotically equivalent.
A genuinely important pitfall, demonstrated by this example The quick Peto-approximation calculator on this page gives HR ≈ 0.60, p ≈ 0.48 for the same data — noticeably different from the true Cox MLE above (HR 0.50, p 0.31). Both point the same direction and both lack significance, but the magnitude differs meaningfully. Quick observed/expected approximations like Peto's are useful for sanity checks and back-of-envelope grant calculations, but they are not a substitute for fitting the actual model on patient-level data, especially with small numbers of events — exactly the regime most early-phase oncology trials operate in.

Interpreting Cox model output line by line

Output elementWhat it means
coef (β)Log hazard ratio. Sign indicates direction (negative = lower hazard = better); magnitude isn't intuitively interpretable on its own — exponentiate it.
exp(coef)The hazard ratio itself. <1 favours the higher-coded group (e.g. armB here); >1 favours the reference.
se(coef)Standard error of β, on the log scale — needed to build the CI for the HR (always exponentiate the CI bounds of β, never the SE directly).

Concordance / C-indexHow well the model discriminates who has the event sooner — analogous to AUC for binary outcomes. 0.5 = no better than chance, 1.0 = perfect discrimination.

Checking the proportional hazards assumption

Before trusting a single HR, check whether it's actually constant over follow-up:

  • Schoenfeld residuals — the standard formal test (e.g. cox.zph() in R). A significant trend in residuals over time signals non-proportional hazards.
  • Log(−log(S(t))) plots — under proportional hazards, curves for different groups should be roughly parallel; converging, diverging, or crossing lines suggest the assumption is violated.
  • Visual inspection of the KM curves themselves — crossing curves are a strong visual cue that a single HR will misrepresent the relationship (e.g. common with immunotherapy, where an early hazard disadvantage can be followed by long-term benefit).

If hazards are clearly non-proportional, consider: a model with a time-varying coefficient or a time-by-treatment interaction term; splitting the analysis into pre-specified time intervals; or reporting RMST instead of a single HR (07e) — which doesn't require the proportional hazards assumption at all.

Multivariable Cox models Everything above generalises directly to multiple covariates: h(t|X) = h₀(t)·exp(β₁X₁+β₂X₂+...). Each HR is then interpreted as the effect of that one covariate holding all others constant — the same adjustment logic as multivariable linear or logistic regression, just applied to the hazard. See the Confounding tool (09) for guidance on which covariates belong in the adjustment set in the first place.

Restricted Mean Survival Time & Landmark Analysis

Both methods on this page exist to handle situations where the standard hazard ratio is either unreliable (non-proportional hazards) or not the question you actually want answered (clinicians and patients often want a clinically grounded number, not an abstract ratio).

Restricted Mean Survival Time (RMST)

RMST is the area under the KM curve up to a pre-specified time horizon τ — equivalently, the average event-free time a patient experiences during that window. Unlike the hazard ratio, RMST requires no proportional hazards assumption at all, and unlike median survival, it uses information from the entire curve up to τ rather than a single point.

Restricted mean survival time
RMST(τ) = ∫0τ Ŝ(t) dt   — the area under the KM curve from 0 to τ

How to phrase this for a clinical audience "Over the first 18 months, patients on Treatment spent on average 2.4 more months alive and progression-free than patients on Control." This is a directly meaningful clinical statement in a way "the hazard ratio was 0.50" rarely is on its own.

Choosing τ

τ must be pre-specified (not chosen after looking at the data) and cannot exceed the shortest follow-up time in either arm with reasonable risk-set size — choosing τ too close to the end of follow-up makes the estimate unstable, for the same reason the right-hand tail of a KM curve is unreliable (see 07b). A common, defensible choice is the minimum of the two arms' maximum follow-up times, or a clinically meaningful milestone (e.g. 2-year DFS in early-stage disease).

Common pitfall Comparing RMST values computed at different τ between studies or arms — the comparison is only valid when τ is identical across the groups being compared, since RMST is mechanically smaller for a smaller τ regardless of the underlying survival experience.

Landmark Analysis

Landmark analysis addresses a different, very common error: comparing groups defined by something that happens after baseline (e.g. "responders vs non-responders to induction chemotherapy") using all the follow-up time from diagnosis. This guarantees responders look artificially better, because by definition they had to survive long enough to be classified as responders — a form of immortal time bias.

Immortal time bias — why this happens If "response" is assessed at 3 months, any patient who dies in month 1 cannot be a responder by definition — they're forced into the "non-responder" group purely by dying early, inflating the apparent survival benefit of "being a responder."

The landmark solution

  1. Choose a landmark time tL (e.g. 3 months) at which the exposure of interest (response status) will be assessed.
  2. Exclude any patient who had the event or was censored before tL — they were never at risk of being correctly classified.
  3. Among patients who survived to tL, classify by status at that landmark, then run Kaplan-Meier/log-rank/Cox as normal, with survival time measured from tL onward (not from baseline).

The trade-off is a smaller, later-starting sample — but it removes the structural bias entirely. The landmark time itself should be chosen on clinical grounds (e.g. when response is conventionally assessed) and pre-specified, not picked to maximise apparent significance.

Related, more flexible alternative For exposures that can change at any time (not just at one fixed landmark), a time-varying covariate in an extended Cox model handles the same problem more generally — see the time-dependent covariate note on the Competing Risks page (07f) advanced section.

Competing Risks & the Fine-Gray Model

A competing risk is an event that prevents the event of interest from ever happening. In oncology this is everywhere: if you're studying local recurrence, death from any other cause before recurrence is a competing risk — that patient can never go on to recur, because they're no longer alive to do so. Treating a competing event as ordinary censoring (the default behaviour if you don't explicitly handle it) is one of the most common and most consequential errors in oncology survival analysis.

Why "treat death as censoring" is wrong

Standard KM censoring assumes that, had follow-up continued, the censored patient would eventually have had the event of interest at some point — that's the entire justification for KM's risk-set logic. A patient who died of an unrelated cause will never recur. Folding them into the censored group as if they might still recur later systematically inflates the apparent risk of the event of interest, especially when the competing event is common (e.g. older patients, multimorbid populations, or long follow-up windows where age-related mortality accumulates).

Numerical illustration

A simple example with constant cause-specific hazards (recurrence hazard 0.05/year, competing-death hazard 0.08/year) shows the scale of the problem:

The scale of the error By year 20, the naive method estimates a 64% chance of recurrence; the true cumulative incidence is only 36% — an absolute overstatement of 28 percentage points, purely from mishandling the competing risk of death. This isn't a small-sample quirk; it's a structural bias that gets worse the longer the follow-up and the more common the competing event.

Two correct frameworks, two different questions

The literature distinguishes two hazard concepts, and — critically — they answer different questions and can point in different directions for the same data:

Cause-specific hazard modelFine-Gray subdistribution hazard model
Risk set at time tOnly those still event-free for every causeIncludes those who already had a competing event
Question answered"Etiologic": instantaneous rate of the event of interest, among those who could still have it"Prognostic": how covariates affect the actual cumulative incidence (real-world probability) of the event

A genuinely important real-world consequence A treatment can reduce the cause-specific hazard of recurrence (e.g. because surgery removes the tumour more completely) while simultaneously not changing or even worsening the cumulative incidence of recurrence — if that same treatment carries higher competing mortality risk that "protects" patients from ever surviving long enough to recur. Reporting only the cause-specific HR in this situation can be clinically misleading about real-world prognosis; reporting the subdistribution HR (or, better, both side by side) avoids this trap.

The cumulative incidence function (CIF)

Relationship between cause-specific hazard and CIF
Fk(t) = ∫0t hk(u) × S(u) du
— the probability of having had cause-k event by time t equals the cause-k hazard, weighted by the probability of having survived (event-free of any cause) to that moment

This formula is exactly why the naive method fails: it implicitly drops the S(u) term that accounts for competing events removing patients from the at-risk population over time.

Reporting standards

  • State explicitly which competing events were included and how they were handled.
  • Present cumulative incidence curves (not naive KM curves) for outcomes with meaningful competing risks — e.g. cancer-specific death with competing all-cause non-cancer death, recurrence with competing death.
  • If reporting a hazard ratio, specify whether it's cause-specific or subdistribution — the two are not interchangeable and most readers will assume cause-specific unless told otherwise.

A related, separately important issue: time-varying covariates

Some covariates change value during follow-up (e.g. CRP rises before recurrence is detected; a patient's performance status declines). Using their value at study entry in a standard Cox model is a form of model misspecification — and using a value measured after the time point being analysed (e.g. a biomarker drawn at recurrence, plugged in as if known from baseline) reintroduces the same kind of immortal-time bias discussed under landmark analysis (07e). The general solution is a Cox model with time-varying covariates, where each patient contributes multiple time-intervals with the covariate value that was actually true during each interval (e.g. survival::coxph() with start-stop interval data in R) — conceptually a generalisation of the landmark idea to a covariate that can change repeatedly rather than at one fixed point.

Cure Models & Frailty (Random Effects) Models

Both methods on this page relax an assumption the standard Cox model makes implicitly: that every patient is drawn from the same hazard process, and that everyone is eventually "susceptible" to the event if followed long enough. Neither is always true in oncology.

Part 1 — Mixture Cure Models

If a meaningful fraction of patients are effectively cured — they will never recur or die of the disease, no matter how long they're followed — the standard KM curve develops a plateau rather than continuing toward zero. A standard Cox model, which assumes everyone is susceptible, doesn't represent this well: the hazard ratio it estimates is a blend of "effect on whether someone is cured" and "effect on how fast non-cured patients progress," which can obscure clinically distinct mechanisms.

Mixture cure model — population survival function
Spop(t) = π + (1 − π) × Su(t)
π = cure fraction (proportion never susceptible to the event); Su(t) = survival function among the uncured/susceptible subgroup, Su(∞)=0

The model is fitted as two linked sub-models:

  • Incidence part — a logistic regression for π itself: logit(π) = z'γ, modelling which covariates predict being cured.
  • Latency part — a standard survival model (often Cox-type) for Su(t) among the uncured: S_u(t) = S_u0(t)^exp(x'β), modelling how fast the disease progresses among those who are not cured.
Relevance to early-onset colorectal cancer research Mixture cure models have been applied directly to colorectal cancer survival data to separate short-term and long-term survivorship effects — disentangling "this treatment increases the chance of being cured outright" from "this treatment slows progression among those who will eventually recur," two quite different clinical claims that an ordinary Cox HR collapses into one number.

When a cure model is — and isn't — appropriate

  • Use one when: the KM curve visibly plateaus at a non-zero level with adequate follow-up and risk-set size at the plateau (not just censoring near the end masquerading as a plateau — check the at-risk table).
  • Don't use one when: follow-up is too short to distinguish a true cure plateau from "these patients just haven't recurred yet" — cure models extrapolate beyond observed data and that extrapolation is only trustworthy with mature follow-up.

Part 2 — Frailty (Random Effects) Models

The standard Cox model assumes patients are statistically independent. This breaks down whenever outcomes are clustered — multiple patients treated at the same centre, by the same surgeon, or in repeated/multiple events per patient. Patients within a cluster often share unmeasured factors (surgical skill, local pathology turnaround time, supportive care quality) that make their outcomes correlated, even after adjusting for measured covariates.

Shared frailty Cox model
hij(t) = h₀(t) × ui × exp(β'Xij)
i = cluster (e.g. centre), j = patient within cluster; ui is a cluster-level random effect (frailty), typically assumed Gamma-distributed with mean 1

ui > 1 means that cluster has systematically higher hazard than average (worse outcomes), unexplained by the measured covariates; ui < 1 means better-than-average outcomes. The variance of ui quantifies how much unexplained heterogeneity exists between clusters — useful in its own right as a measure of, e.g., between-centre variation in a multi-centre trial.

Frailty vs. stratification — two different fixes for the same problem

Stratified Cox modelShared frailty model
ApproachSeparate, unrelated baseline hazard per clusterSingle baseline hazard, shared multiplicative random effect per cluster
Assumes clusters are...Entirely distinct, no shared structureDrawn from a common population of possible clusters
Can estimate centre effect size?No (effect absorbed into separate baselines)Yes (frailty variance is itself a reportable, interpretable quantity)
Best when...Few clusters, each with many patientsMany clusters (e.g. multi-centre trials, registries), borrowing strength across them
Why this matters for multi-centre oncology trials Ignoring centre-level clustering when it's present typically produces standard errors that are too small — the model effectively (and wrongly) treats correlated patients as independent extra information, making results look more precise and more significant than they really are. This is a particular risk in trials run across centres with very different patient volumes, where a handful of large centres can dominate the apparent treatment effect.

Practical fitting notes

  • R: coxph(Surv(time,event) ~ x + frailty(centre), data=df) or the more actively maintained coxme / frailtyEM packages for shared gamma or log-normal frailty.
  • Gamma and log-normal frailty distributions usually give very similar substantive conclusions in practice — the choice rarely matters as much as whether to include frailty at all.
  • With very few clusters (say, under 5–10 centres), frailty variance estimates become unstable — a stratified model or simply adjusting for centre as a fixed effect may be more robust in that situation.
Where this connects elsewhere in the booklet The same clustering logic that motivates frailty models here is the survival-specific version of the general repeated-measures/clustering problem — compare with the mixed-effects approach to repeated continuous measures discussed under Hypothesis Testing (04) and the broader confounding/causal structure questions in the Confounder Classifier tool (09).

Regression: Quantifying a Relationship, Not Just Detecting One

Where a correlation or a t-test tells you whether two things are related, regression gives you an actual equation: how much does the outcome change for a one-unit change in the predictor, and how confident can you be in that number?

Worked example

Linear regression relating a biomarker level (x) to % tumour size change (y) in 12 patients:

ResultValue
Slope−7.66 (95% CI −9.95 to −5.37)
Intercept14.86
R²0.847
p-value<0.0001

Each 1-unit rise in biomarker level is associated with a 7.66-point greater reduction in tumour size, on average. At a biomarker level of 3.0, the model predicts roughly −8.1% change. R²=0.847 means about 85% of the variation in tumour size change is explained by this one biomarker — a strong relationship, though real clinical data this clean is unusual outside a deliberately illustrative example.

What to watch out for

  • Correlation in a regression line is not causation. The biomarker might genuinely drive tumour shrinkage, or both might simply be downstream effects of disease severity or another shared cause — regression alone can't distinguish these.
  • Always plot the data before trusting R² and a p-value. A single outlier can create or destroy an apparently strong linear relationship; the summary statistics alone won't reveal this.
  • Extrapolating beyond the observed range of x is unreliable. A model fit on biomarker levels 1.5–4.5 says nothing trustworthy about a patient with a level of 15.
  • A high R² with a small sample (here, n=12) can still be fragile — wide confidence intervals on the slope are the honest signal of how much the estimate could shift with more data.
  • Check the residuals, not just R². A curved or fan-shaped pattern in the residuals signals the straight-line model doesn't actually fit, even when R² looks respectable.

Confounding: When the Raw Comparison Tells the Opposite Story

A confounder distorts the apparent relationship between an exposure and an outcome because it's linked to both. Left unadjusted, it can not only weaken a true effect — it can reverse its apparent direction entirely.

Worked example

Comparing mortality after two surgical approaches, Approach A is used more often in older, higher-risk patients:

Approach AApproach B
Under 65 (mortality)5.0% (n=100)6.0% (n=300)
65 and over (mortality)20.0% (n=300)22.0% (n=100)
Crude (unadjusted) mortality16.2%10.0%
Age-standardised mortality12.5%14.0%
The reversal, explained Within every single age group, Approach A has lower mortality than B. But because A was used far more often in older, higher-risk patients, the crude comparison makes A look worse overall (16.2% vs 10.0%) — the exact opposite of the truth. Adjusting for age (the confounder) reverses the conclusion entirely. This is a textbook case of Simpson's paradox, and it's not a rare or contrived phenomenon — it happens routinely whenever treatment allocation correlates with severity.

What to watch out for

  • Always ask why patients ended up in each group. If sicker patients were more likely to receive one treatment, any unadjusted comparison is suspect from the outset.
  • A confounder must affect both exposure and outcome. A variable that only predicts the outcome (not who gets which treatment) is a precision variable, not a confounder — adjusting for it helps precision without correcting bias.
  • Don't adjust for a mediator — a variable that exposure causes, which then causes the outcome. Adjusting for it blocks part of the very effect you're trying to measure (see the Confounder Classifier tool, 09, for how to tell the difference).
  • Stratified or standardised analysis can reveal a reversal that the crude number completely hides — always check subgroup-specific results, not just the pooled estimate, when allocation looks non-random.
  • Use the Confounder/Mediator/Collider Classifier tool in this section to reason through whether a specific variable belongs in your adjustment set.

Meta-analysis: Combining Studies Without Fooling Yourself

A meta-analysis pools effect estimates from multiple studies into one, more precise number — but only when those studies are estimating a similar enough underlying effect that combining them is meaningful in the first place.

Worked example

Five trials of the same drug, each reporting an odds ratio for treatment response with its 95% CI:

TrialOR95% CI
Trial A1.81.1–2.9
Trial B2.11.3–3.4
Trial C1.30.8–2.1
Trial D2.51.5–4.2
Trial E1.61.0–2.6
Pooled (fixed-effect)1.801.45–2.24

Heterogeneity here is low (I²=0%) — all five trials' individual confidence intervals overlap substantially, and the pooled estimate is noticeably more precise (a narrower CI) than any single trial alone. This is meta-analysis working as intended: a clearer, more confident answer than any one study could provide.

What to watch out for

  • Check I² before trusting the pooled number. High heterogeneity (commonly flagged above ~50%) means the studies may be answering subtly different questions — pooling them into one number can obscure more than it reveals.
  • A forest plot's diamond width is the pooled confidence interval — if it crosses the line of no effect (OR=1 or RR=1), the pooled result isn't statistically significant, even if most individual studies point the same direction.
  • Publication bias is invisible in the forest plot itself. A funnel plot, or a formal test like Egger's, is needed to check whether small "negative" studies are simply missing from the literature.
  • Fixed-effect and random-effects models can give quite different answers when heterogeneity is real — random-effects widens the CI to reflect genuine between-study variation, and is usually the more defensible default outside very homogeneous trial sets.
  • Use the Meta-analysis Pooling Calculator in this section to pool your own studies' estimates and see I² calculated directly.

Translational Statistics: Validating a Biomarker Properly

A promising biomarker that separates two groups in a discovery dataset still needs to clear several further hurdles before it's clinically useful — reproducibility, an appropriate cutoff, and validation in a genuinely independent sample.

Worked example

A candidate biomarker measured in 15 treatment responders and 15 non-responders:

GroupMean biomarker level
Responders (n=15)45.0
Non-responders (n=15)26.6
Welch's t-testAUC (discrimination)
t=7.10, p<0.00010.967

An AUC of 0.967 indicates excellent discrimination between responders and non-responders in this dataset — but that number describes how well the biomarker separates groups that have already had their outcome observed. It says nothing yet about how the biomarker would perform prospectively, in a new cohort, or whether a specific cutoff generalises beyond this sample.

What to watch out for

  • A biomarker discovered and "validated" in the same dataset is not validated. AUC and cutoffs derived from one sample are almost always optimistic when reapplied to fresh data — a genuinely independent validation cohort is essential before clinical claims.
  • Choosing a cutoff after seeing the data ("data-driven cutoffs") inflates apparent performance. Pre-specify a cutoff from prior literature or a separate training set wherever possible.
  • Batch effects and assay drift are a common, under-appreciated source of false "biomarker" signal — particularly in -omics work, where technical/processing differences between groups can masquerade as biology.
  • Multiple candidate biomarkers screened together need multiple-testing correction (see 05) before any single one is taken forward — this is exactly the genomics scenario in the multiple-testing worked example.
  • Statistical significance in a small discovery cohort (here, n=30) is a hypothesis to test further, not a finished result — treat early biomarker statistics as hypothesis-generating until replicated.
